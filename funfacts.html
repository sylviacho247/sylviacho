Fun Facts About Me (You Never Knew You Needed)

  • 🏔️ I spent my early childhood in Banff, Alberta — a tiny town nestled in the Canadian Rockies, full of elk, tourists, and accidental linguistic fieldwork. As the only ethnically Korean kid in my grade, I became keenly aware of how heritage languages shape speech (and got very good at explaining Korea to confused classmates).

  • 🍓 I spent most of my life in BC’s Fraser Valley — aka the berry capital of the province — where my summers were fueled by an unshakable ambition to become the fastest berry picker west of the Rockies. I may not have broken any speed records, but I did eat a concerning number of blueberries on the job.

  • ⛸️ I’m a proud Canadian from the Rockies, but I have a confession: I’m *terrible* at ice skating, skiing, snowboarding, tobogganing… basically all winter sports. I’ve accepted that my true talents lie indoors — with language data and hot chocolate.

  • 🗣️ Out of four siblings, I’m the only one who can fluently speak, read, write, and understand Korean. I never brag about it at family dinners... but I definitely feel a secret sense of pride — especially when I can read academic linguistics papers in Korean like it’s no big deal.

  • ✈️ I didn’t own a passport until after undergrad. Turns out, you don’t need one if you never leave the North American continent! I eventually got my very first passport to study in Korea for my master’s and to learn about my heritage culture.

  • 🦅 I have a deep love for Canadian Indigenous languages and include them in almost every course that I teach. They’re not only beautiful and complex, but also powerful vessels of culture, history, and resilience. And yes, I absolutely geek out over their rich consonant inventories, often featuring ejectives, uvulars, and fascinating contrasts like plain vs. glottalized vs. labialized voiceless plosives.

  • 🧸 Last but certainly not least, I’m a very proud aunt to the *cutest* nephew and niece. They speak only English, yet somehow understand all the Korean their grandparents throw at them — like tiny secret agents decoding grandparent code. Watching them acquire speech and language has been a linguist’s dream come true… basically live fieldwork, but with more snacks.
